# Rosenshine's 10 Principles of Instruction

> Research-based instructional strategies for effective teaching (Rosenshine, 2012).

---

## Overview

**Foundation:** 30+ years of classroom research + cognitive science

**Goal:** Maximize learning through systematic, explicit instruction.

**Application:** Lesson structure, explanation design, practice activities.

---

## The 10 Principles

### 1. Begin a lesson with a short review of previous learning

**Why:** Strengthens connections, activates prior knowledge, reveals gaps.

**How:**
- Quick quiz on previous lesson (2-3 questions)
- Verbal review of key concepts (1-2 minutes)
- Correct errors BEFORE introducing new material

**Example:**
- "Before we learn about germane load, let's recall: What are the other two types of cognitive load?"

---

### 2. Present new material in small steps with student practice after each step

**Why:** Limits cognitive overload, allows processing before adding more.

**How:**
- Break complex skill into 3-5 sub-steps
- Teach one step, practice that step, THEN next step
- Check understanding after each chunk

**Example:**
- Teaching lesson design: Step 1 = Hook (teach + practice) → Step 2 = Core Concept (teach + practice) → Step 3 = Explanation (teach + practice)

---

### 3. Ask a large number of questions and check the responses of all students

**Why:** Reveals understanding (or misunderstanding) immediately, maintains engagement.

**How:**
- Ask questions every 2-3 minutes
- Use mix of recall, comprehension, application questions
- Check multiple students, not just volunteers
- Use polling, written responses, or quick verbal checks

**Example:**
- "How many of you can name all 6 Bloom levels? Write them down now. Compare with your neighbor."

---

### 4. Provide models and worked examples

**Why:** Reduces cognitive load, shows expert thinking process.

**How:**
- Show complete example BEFORE student attempts
- Think aloud while demonstrating
- Use multiple examples (not just one)

**Example:**
- "Here's a lesson I designed using the 7-part model. Notice how I structured the hook to match awareness level 3..."

---

### 5. Guide student practice

**Why:** Scaffolds learning, catches errors early, builds confidence.

**How:**
- Students attempt with instructor support available
- Provide hints, not answers
- Gradually reduce assistance
- Immediate corrective feedback

**Example:**
- "Now design your hook. I'll walk around and give feedback. Start with the ARCS checklist."

---

### 6. Check for student understanding

**Why:** Prevents building on shaky foundation, reveals hidden confusion.

**How:**
- Ask students to explain in their own words
- Request examples/non-examples
- Observe practice attempts
- Use formative assessments

**Example:**
- "Explain to your partner what 'extraneous load' means using an example from your own experience."

---

### 7. Obtain a high success rate

**Why:** Success builds confidence and motivation (ARCS Confidence dimension).

**How:**
- Aim for 80%+ success during guided practice
- Adjust difficulty if success rate drops
- Provide sufficient scaffolding
- Break down further if needed

**Example:**
- If most students struggle with hook design, add more structure: "Use this 4-question template to build your hook."

---

### 8. Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks

**Why:** Enables success on complex tasks before full independence.

**How:**
- Checklists, templates, guides
- Sentence starters, outlines
- Step-by-step procedures
- Gradually remove scaffolds as competence grows

**Example:**
- Provide 7-part lesson template with prompts for each section, then remove prompts, then remove structure.

---

### 9. Require and monitor independent practice

**Why:** Automation requires extensive practice, reveals true mastery.

**How:**
- Assign practice that requires retrieval (not reference)
- Monitor accuracy and fluency
- Provide feedback on independent work
- Continue until automatic

**Example:**
- "Design a complete lesson without referring to the model. Submit for feedback."

---

### 10. Engage students in weekly and monthly review

**Why:** Spacing effect enhances long-term retention (Bjork), prevents forgetting.

**How:**
- Review key concepts weekly (short quiz or discussion)
- Cumulative assessments monthly
- Mix old and new material
- Use retrieval practice (not re-reading)

**Example:**
- "This week's quiz includes 2 questions from this lesson + 3 from previous lessons."

## Application to 7-Part Lesson Model

| Lesson Section | Rosenshine Principles Applied |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| **Hook Initial** | #1 (Review prior learning) |
| **Core Concept** | #2 (Present in small steps) |
| **Explanation** | #2 (Small steps), #3 (Questions), #6 (Check understanding) |
| **Analogies & Examples** | #4 (Provide models) |

## Quick Reference: When to Use Each Principle

| Situation | Principle(s) |
|-----------|-------------|
| Starting a lesson | #1 (Review) |
| Introducing complex concept | #2 (Small steps), #8 (Scaffolds) |
| During explanation | #3 (Questions), #6 (Check understanding) |
| Before student attempts | #4 (Models/examples) |
| First student attempt | #5 (Guided practice), #7 (High success rate) |
| When students struggle | #8 (Scaffolds), #2 (Smaller steps) |
| Practice without support | #9 (Independent practice) |
| Preventing forgetting | #10 (Spaced review) |

## Common Implementation Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| Present all material, then practice at end | Cognitive overload, delayed feedback | #2: Small steps + practice after EACH step |
| Only ask volunteers questions | Miss students who don't understand | #3: Check ALL students (polls, written, random) |
| Skip worked examples | Students don't know what "good" looks like | #4: Show complete example before practice |
| Throw students into independent work too soon | Frustration, errors become habits | #5: Guided practice BEFORE independent |
| Don't check understanding during lesson | Build on faulty foundation | #6: Frequent checks (every 3-5 min) |
| Allow 50% success rate during practice | Demotivation, inefficient learning | #7: Adjust difficulty to reach 80%+ success |
| Give complex task without support | Overwhelming, high failure rate | #8: Provide scaffolds (templates, checklists) |
| No spaced review | Forgetting, poor retention | #10: Weekly/monthly cumulative review |

## Integration with Other Frameworks

**Rosenshine + Mayer:**
- Rosenshine #2 (small steps) + Mayer Segmenting Principle
- Rosenshine #4 (models) + Mayer Multimedia Principle

**Rosenshine + Bloom:**
- Rosenshine #3 (questions) should span Bloom levels (Remember → Apply)
- Rosenshine #9 (independent practice) targets Apply/Analyze levels

**Rosenshine + Bjork:**
- Rosenshine #10 (spaced review) = Bjork Spacing Effect
- Rosenshine #9 (independent practice) = Bjork Retrieval Practice

## Checklist (Every Lesson)

- [ ] Starts with review of prior learning (#1)
- [ ] New material presented in small steps (#2)
- [ ] Includes frequent questions checking all students (#3)
- [ ] Provides worked examples before practice (#4)
- [ ] Includes guided practice with feedback (#5)
- [ ] Checks understanding multiple times (#6)
- [ ] Guided practice success rate 80%+ (#7)
- [ ] Scaffolds provided for complex tasks (#8)
- [ ] Includes independent practice (#9)
- [ ] Connects to spaced review system (#10)
